
## Slide 1 — Title (MLION)

"Good morning everyone, and thank you for being here. Today I'd like to walk you through a project we've been working on called MLION — Machine Learning Incident-Ops Navigator. The idea is to bring Operational Risk Intelligence to our incident data using Natural Language Processing. Let me take you through what the project is about, why it matters, and the preparatory work we've already done."

## Slide 2 — Project Proposal

"Let me start with the proposal itself, framed in three parts: scope, goal, and ambition.

The **scope** is our Operational Risk historical incidents — specifically the unstructured, free-text data that today is largely underexploited.

The **goal** is to explore this data with an innovative approach based on Machine Learning techniques, rather than treating these descriptions as text we simply store and never analyze.

And the **ambition** is twofold: first, to acquire additional knowledge about our incidents and losses — knowledge that's currently locked inside the text — and second, to contribute to identifying and developing augmented risk mitigation measures. In other words, not just understanding the past better, but actively helping prevent future losses."

## Slide 3 — Project Context

"To give you the context: our Operational Risk data lives in RISK360, and it comes in the form of three related datasets — Historical Incidents, Causes, and Consequences. Historical Incidents capture the core event — the 'when' and 'where' of a failure. Causes identify the underlying triggers. And Consequences capture the financial and non-financial impacts.

Each of these contains two kinds of data. There's **structured data** — dates, loss amounts, business unit codes, Basel event types — which is essential for regulatory reporting and which we already use well. But there's also **unstructured data**, and this is where the opportunity is. The description field is especially valuable: it captures context, patterns, and root causes that the structured fields alone simply miss. That's the data we want to unlock."

## Slide 4 — Academic Validation

"Now, during my research, I came across a paper that essentially answers the exact question we're asking. It's a 2023 paper by a team of mathematicians from universities in Italy, together with operational risk analysts from UniCredit — a major Italian credit institution. In it, they apply text analysis and clustering techniques to operational risk loss descriptions — which is precisely what we want to do.

This matters for three reasons. First, it **confirms the relevance** of our idea — it validates that unstructured descriptions are a primary source of intelligence for root-cause analysis. Second, it **gives us credibility** — we're not improvising; we have a robust, peer-reviewed foundation for our internal risk analytics initiative. And third, it gives us a **structured methodology** — a proven workflow going from cleaning, to vectorization, to clustering — which ensures consistency in how we implement this. So this discovery essentially de-risks our approach before we even start."

## Slide 5 — Proposed Workflow (1/2)

"Here's the end-to-end workflow we're proposing. It's a nine-step pipeline.

We start with **data ingestion** — joining the Historical Incidents, Causes, and Consequences datasets. Then **text cleaning** — normalizing the text through lowercasing, stop-word removal, and lemmatization. Step three is **vectorization** — converting the cleaned text into a word-frequency matrix. Step four, **semantic enrichment** — enriching that matrix with synonym and semantic-similarity information, so that words meaning the same thing are treated as related. Step five, **2D projection** — projecting the data into two dimensions so we can visually explore the clusters. Step six, **cluster labeling** — identifying and labeling the granular root-cause clusters.

And then, beyond the core analysis, steps seven to nine show where this can go: an **auto-classifier** to automatically tag new incidents, a **risk-score model** combining text with structured fields for predictive scoring, and finally **APIs and dashboards** to expose the results in real time."

## Slide 6 — Proposed Workflow (2/2)

"To make this concrete and visual: what we're really doing is taking **unstructured data** — these long, messy incident descriptions, like the one you see on the left — feeding them through an **NLP model**, and getting out **clusters of events** on the right.

Each point is an incident; each color is a cluster of incidents that share a common root cause. So instead of reading thousands of descriptions one by one, an analyst can see groups emerge — and tag them, for example, as 'credit card forgery,' or 'disputes on interest rate calculation.' That's the core value: turning text into discoverable, granular root-cause groups."

## Slide 7 — Potential Business Outcomes

"So why does this matter for the business? Ultimately, it transforms our Operational Risk data from a static reporting archive into a preventive intelligence engine. And that splits into two families of outcomes.

On the **intelligence** side: we can discover granular root-cause groups that today are invisible, automatically tag new incidents as they come in, and identify hidden failure patterns across the bank.

On the **prevention** side: once we understand these patterns, we can predict severity and likelihood, generate early-warning risk scores, and ultimately support mitigation strategy with evidence. So we move from describing what happened, to anticipating what might happen next."

## Slide 8 — Preparatory Works (intro slide)

"Now, before building the full pipeline, we did a significant amount of preparatory work on the 2025 validated incidents to make sure the data foundation is solid. Let me show you what we found and how we handled it."

## Slide 9 — Managing 1-to-N Relationships

"The first challenge is structural. Our three datasets have a one-to-many relationship: a single incident can have several causes and several consequences.

Looking at the 2025 validated incidents — that's 10,105 incidents — we analyzed the distribution. For **causes**: about 89% of incidents have a single cause, 10% have two, and only around 1% have three or more. For **consequences**, it's a bit more spread out: about 73% have a single consequence, but 21% have two, and the rest have three or more.

This matters because if we naively joined the three datasets together, an incident with, say, two causes and two consequences would be duplicated into four rows — which would distort the loss amounts, inflate our counts, and contaminate the clustering. So we had to handle this carefully."

## Slide 10 — Aggregation Resolution

"The solution was aggregation. We aggregated the causes and consequences per incident, so that each incident remains a single row — preserving the unicity of the record.

And critically, we applied **field-specific logic** for each aggregation. For example, for the loss amounts in the consequences, we summed them — and we verified that this sum matches the total loss field in the Historical Incidents dataset, which gives us a strong consistency check. We did the equivalent appropriate aggregation for every field — counts, categories, text — depending on its meaning.

The result: a clean, consistent dataset of 10,105 incidents, each represented by a single comprehensive record, while preserving all the granular detail. This is exactly the foundation we need for the downstream NLP."

## Slide 11 — Multilingual Incident Descriptions

"The second challenge we discovered is that our incident descriptions are multilingual. When we ran language detection, we found the corpus is roughly 49% English — that's about 4,976 incidents — around 43% French, about 4,379 incidents, roughly 7% Italian, and a negligible tail of other languages.

This has an important implication. Because nearly half the data is non-English, we can't take an English-only approach — we'd be throwing away half our incidents, and those incidents almost certainly correspond to specific entities and geographies. So to cluster everything in a single, unified semantic space, we need to translate everything into English.

The complication is that, in our Dataiku environment, we don't have the translation plugin available. So we had to find a workaround."

## Slide 12 — Translation Workaround

"And here's the workaround we built. Within our Dataiku flow, we split out the non-English descriptions — the French and the Italian — into separate files. We then used the CIB AI Platform's Translate tool to translate them into English: that's about 4,379 French and 684 Italian descriptions, roughly 5,000 in total, processed in under four hours.

Once translated, we brought those English versions back into the flow and rejoined them with the master dataset — matching on the incident ID, so every incident lines up exactly with its original structured data. The result is a single, fully English corpus, ready for the NLP pipeline, without ever having to send our data outside the bank's controlled environment."
