
## Why the paper's reasoning matters for your data prep

The paper builds a six-step workflow (cleaning → vectorization → semantic adjustment → dimensionality reduction → cluster selection → cluster validation) and applies it to the UniCredit CoRep C17.02 dataset — 644 events of the Basel type "Clients, Products & Business Practices", with descriptions capped at 250 characters. Several choices in that paper directly constrain how you should prepare your three-dataset foundation:

**Cleaning is not cosmetic, it changes results.** The authors strip stop-words, punctuation, digits, special characters, lowercase everything, and lemmatize. The reason: the Bag-of-Words representation treats every distinct token as a separate dimension, so "Client", "clients", and "client." would each become their own column in a 1037-wide dictionary. Without cleaning, the document-by-term matrix becomes sparse noise.

**TF beats TF-IDF on short texts — this is critical for you.** The paper explicitly chooses TF over TF-IDF because their descriptions are short (≤250 chars) and the informative words for cluster 1 ("interest", "rates", "calculation") appear in nearly all cluster-1 descriptions. IDF would penalize exactly those words and collapse the cluster into the origin (their Figure 11 shows accuracy dropping from 96% to 68%). **Implication for your prep:** you need to measure the length distribution of your text fields to decide whether you're in the "short-text TF regime" or the "longer-document TF-IDF regime" — and your text fields will likely span both regimes (incident descriptions vs. cause descriptions vs. consequence descriptions may behave very differently).

**Semantic adjustment via word2vec rescues synonymy.** Their "customer lost his card" vs "client mislaid her card" example shows cosine similarity jumping from 0.4 to 0.99 after word-embedding adjustment with a 0.8 similarity threshold. The threshold matters: 0.7 introduces noise, 0.9 does almost nothing. **Implication:** you'll want a single, consolidated text field per incident (or a small number of well-named text fields), because running word2vec separately on three disjoint text columns wastes the cross-field semantic signal. Concatenating the incident description, aggregated causes, and aggregated consequences gives word2vec a richer co-occurrence context.

**Dimensionality reduction via LSA/SVD has a sign problem.** The paper notes that Mixtures of Unigrams, Deep MoU, Dirichlet-Multinomial Mixtures, and LDA cannot operate on the LSA 2D output because LSA produces negative values; they must be applied to the (non-negative) document-by-term matrix directly. **Implication:** keep the cleaned-but-not-yet-vectorized text available downstream — don't vectorize prematurely in the prep phase.

**The analyst-in-the-loop is part of the methodology.** Cluster selection (step 5) is human-driven on the 2D plot; clustering algorithms (step 6) validate the human choice. So your prep dataset must be queryable by analysts: every row needs to be traceable back to a single incident, with the original descriptions preserved alongside cleaned versions.

The Cartesian multiplication problem you identified is therefore not just a hygiene issue — it would corrupt every downstream step: TF counts inflated by duplication, LSA dimensions distorted, k-means centroids pulled toward over-represented incidents, and silhouette indices artificially inflated.

With that grounding, here is the step-by-step Dataiku build.

---

## 1. Inspect and profile the 3 raw datasets

Once the three CSV/Excel files are uploaded as Dataiku datasets (call them `incidents_raw`, `causes_raw`, `consequences_raw`), don't rush to build recipes. Open each dataset and use Dataiku's built-in profiling.

For each dataset, click the **Explore** tab first. Look at the inferred storage type and meaning for each column — Dataiku often mis-infers IDs as integers (losing leading zeros) or amounts as strings (because of thousands separators). Then go to the **Status > Schema** tab to see the full type list, and use **Statistics** (the "Worksheets" feature in newer versions, or the column-header histogram in Explore) to get per-column profiles: count, distinct count, missing count, min/max for numerics, top categories for strings.

The four diagnostics you absolutely need before deciding anything:

- **Row count** of each raw dataset.
- **Distinct count of Incident ID** in each — for `incidents_raw` this should equal the row count; for `causes_raw` and `consequences_raw` it should be lower (one-to-many).
- **Average and max number of causes per incident** and same for consequences. Run a quick Group recipe in "preview" mode on `causes_raw` grouping by Incident ID with count, then look at the distribution. If you see incidents with 20+ causes, your text aggregation strategy needs to handle that without creating a 5000-token concatenated string.
- **Length distribution of every free-text field** — character count and word count percentiles (p50, p90, p99). This tells you whether you're in TF or TF-IDF territory and whether truncation is needed.

Document everything in a short markdown wiki page in the Dataiku project (Project home → Wiki). This becomes your data dictionary and saves hours when you revisit the project in three months.

## 2. Decide which structured columns to keep or remove

For the OpRisk text-analysis use case, the rule is: keep what supports either (a) the NLP workflow itself, (b) downstream cluster-validation against known categories (the paper validates clusters against analyst tags — you can additionally validate against Basel event type), or (c) traceability back to the source system for audit.

Drop columns that fall into pure operational plumbing: row-creation timestamps from the source ETL, last-modified-by-user fields, internal hash columns, system status flags unrelated to risk, and any column with >95% missing or >99% single-value (these have zero information). Keep, even if they look redundant, anything that an OpRisk analyst would ask about when interpreting a cluster — country, business unit, occurrence date, gross loss — because cluster interpretation in step 5 of the paper's workflow is exactly "what do these incidents have in common beyond the words?".

A practical filter: if you can't articulate in one sentence why a column would help an analyst understand or validate a cluster, drop it.

## 3. Column classification scheme

Apply this taxonomy to each of the three datasets and record it in the wiki. I'll use the names the paper implies plus what you described:

**Mandatory keys** — `Incident ID` everywhere; `Cause ID` in causes; `Consequence ID` in consequences. These must be non-null, unique where expected, and stored as strings (never numeric — leading zeros and future ID format changes will bite you).

**Useful structured OpRisk variables** — Date of Accounting (the paper uses this for the 2018–2021 window), Date of Occurrence, Date of Discovery, Basel event type level 1, internal risk category, business unit, legal entity, country, Gross Loss, Net Loss, Recovery amount, currency. These are kept for cluster interpretation and validation.

**Text fields** — incident description(s), cause description, consequence description. List them explicitly by name in the wiki.

**Useful flags** — booleans like "is_boundary_credit_risk", "is_litigation", "is_settled", "regulatory_reportable". Keep them; they're cheap and they help validate clusters (e.g., does cluster 2 align with the litigation flag?).

**Technical/audit columns to remove** — created_by, created_at_etl, modified_by, modified_at, source_system_batch_id, internal_record_version, soft_delete_flag (after filtering out deleted rows), and any GUID that duplicates Incident ID.

## 4. Create staging datasets using Prepare recipes

Build three parallel staging branches in the Flow. The naming convention I recommend (matches Dataiku community practice of zone-prefixed names):

- `incidents_raw` → Prepare → `incidents_staging`
- `causes_raw` → Prepare → `causes_staging`
- `consequences_raw` → Prepare → `consequences_staging`

In each Prepare recipe, do the column-level work only — no aggregation yet. Each Prepare recipe should be a sequence of small, named steps so that when something goes wrong six months later, you can read the recipe like a changelog.

Order of operations inside each Prepare recipe:

1. Remove technical/audit columns (use "Delete/Keep columns by name").
2. Rename to standard names (next section).
3. Fix storage types (force IDs to string, dates to date, amounts to double).
4. Filter out soft-deleted rows if applicable.
5. Clean IDs, dates, amounts, categories, text — see step 6 below.
6. Add derived indicators (text length, missing flags) — see step 7 below.

## 5. Rename and standardize columns

Pick one convention and apply it everywhere: I suggest snake_case lowercase, prefixed by entity when ambiguous. So:

In `incidents_staging`: `incident_id`, `date_accounting`, `date_occurrence`, `date_discovery`, `basel_event_type_l1`, `risk_category`, `business_unit`, `legal_entity`, `country`, `gross_loss_eur`, `net_loss_eur`, `recovery_eur`, `currency_original`, `incident_description`.

In `causes_staging`: `cause_id`, `incident_id` (same name as in incidents — this is the join key, so consistency is mandatory), `cause_category`, `cause_description`.

In `consequences_staging`: `consequence_id`, `incident_id`, `consequence_category`, `consequence_financial_impact_eur`, `consequence_description`.

The reason for explicit `_eur` suffixes on amounts: the paper's CoRep dataset is in euros; if your bank operates in multiple currencies, you'll thank yourself later for making the unit visible in the column name.

## 6. Clean IDs, dates, amounts, categories, text fields

Within each Prepare recipe, use these specific Dataiku processors:

**IDs** — use "Trim whitespace" then "Find and replace" with regex to strip non-alphanumeric noise if needed. Then a "Filter rows on value" step to drop rows where the ID is empty. Force the storage type to string in the recipe's output schema.

**Dates** — use "Parse date" with the explicit input format (don't trust auto-detect across a multi-year dataset; formats drift). Output to ISO format. Add a validity filter: dates outside [2010-01-01, today] are almost certainly entry errors — flag them with a new boolean column rather than silently dropping, so an analyst can review.

**Amounts** — use "Find and replace" to remove thousands separators (`,` or `.` depending on locale — be careful, this is the most common silent corruption in OpRisk datasets), then "Convert currency" if you have an FX table, otherwise just "Change type to double". Add a sanity filter: gross_loss < 0 should be flagged (recoveries booked as negative losses are a known data-quality issue). For the paper's methodology, the C17.02 threshold is €100,000 — if your bank uses a similar threshold, consider whether sub-threshold incidents should be excluded from the NLP corpus or kept for richer training data; my recommendation is keep them for the corpus (more text helps word2vec) but flag them for cluster validation filtering.

**Categories** — use "Normalize text" (lowercase, strip accents) for category strings, then "Find and replace" to consolidate known variants ("Internal Fraud" / "internal_fraud" / "INT_FRAUD" → `internal_fraud`). Build a small lookup table as a Dataiku editable dataset if there are more than ~10 variants.

**Text fields** — at this prep stage, do only light cleaning, because heavy NLP cleaning (lemmatization, stop-words) belongs in the NLP recipe later, where it should be reproducible and tunable. At staging, do: trim whitespace, collapse multiple spaces to one, replace newlines with spaces, replace null with empty string. Do not lowercase, do not remove punctuation, do not lemmatize yet — you want the original text preserved for the analyst to read in step 5 of the workflow.

## 7. Text-length and missing-text indicators

Inside each Prepare recipe, add for every text field:

- A new column `<field>_char_len` using a Formula step: `length(field)`.
- A new column `<field>_word_count` using `length(split(field, " "))` (acceptable approximation).
- A new column `<field>_is_missing` as a boolean: `field == "" || isnull(field)`.

These are cheap to compute, they let you filter out empty-text incidents from the NLP corpus, and they let you check post-aggregation that the concatenated text isn't blowing up beyond reasonable limits. The paper used 250-char descriptions — if your post-aggregation text averages 5000 characters, you're in a different regime and may need to revisit the TF-vs-TF-IDF decision.

## 8. Aggregate Causes by Incident ID using the Group recipe

This is where you prevent the Cartesian explosion. From `causes_staging`, create a Group recipe with output `causes_aggregated`.

In the Group recipe configuration:

- **Group keys**: `incident_id` (only).
- **Pre-filter** (optional): exclude rows where `cause_description_is_missing` is true, so empty causes don't pollute the concatenation.
- **Aggregations to compute**:
  - `cause_id`: count → produces `cause_count` (rename it).
  - `cause_id`: count distinct → sanity check, should equal count.
  - `cause_category`: concat distinct with separator ` | ` → produces `cause_categories_concat`.
  - `cause_category`: count distinct → produces `cause_categories_distinct_count`.
  - `cause_description`: concat with separator ` ||| ` → produces `cause_descriptions_concat`. Use a triple-pipe or some other unlikely-in-text separator so you can split later if needed.
  - `cause_description_char_len`: sum → produces `cause_total_char_len`.
  - `cause_description_char_len`: max → useful for outlier detection.

The triple-pipe separator matters: when you later run the NLP cleaning, you'll want either to keep the separator (so word2vec doesn't co-occur the last word of cause 1 with the first word of cause 2 — which would be a false co-occurrence) or to replace it with a sentence boundary like `. `.

## 9. Aggregate Consequences by Incident ID using the Group recipe

Same pattern, output `consequences_aggregated`. Group key: `incident_id`. Aggregations:

- `consequence_id`: count → `consequence_count`.
- `consequence_category`: concat distinct with ` | ` → `consequence_categories_concat`.
- `consequence_category`: count distinct → `consequence_categories_distinct_count`.
- `consequence_description`: concat with ` ||| ` → `consequence_descriptions_concat`.
- `consequence_financial_impact_eur`: sum → `consequence_total_financial_impact_eur`. Note: only sum if the source semantics allow it (multiple consequences with their own impacts that should be totalled). If consequences represent alternative scenarios rather than additive impacts, use max instead — confirm with a business analyst before deciding.
- `consequence_financial_impact_eur`: max, min, count where not null — useful diagnostic columns.
- `consequence_description_char_len`: sum → `consequence_total_char_len`.

## 10. Exact Dataiku Group recipe options to use

When you open the Group recipe, you'll see four key configuration areas. Here's exactly what to set:

**Group tab** — add `incident_id` as the group key. Leave "Group all rows" unchecked.

**Pre-filter tab** — optionally filter out rows where the description is empty, as noted above. This is cleaner than filtering after aggregation.

**Aggregations tab** — this is the matrix where rows are columns and columns are aggregation types. For each source column listed above, tick the appropriate boxes (Count, Count distinct, Sum, Max, Min, Concat, Concat distinct, First, Last). For Concat operations, click the gear/settings icon next to the column name to set the **separator** explicitly to ` ||| ` for descriptions and ` | ` for categories. Do not leave the default comma separator — descriptions contain commas, and you'll get an unparseable mess.

**Post-filter tab** — leave empty at this stage.

**Output tab** — give the output dataset a clear name, store it in the same connection as your staging datasets (typically the project's default filesystem or SQL connection). If your data is large (>1M rows of causes), use SQL storage rather than CSV — Group recipes execute much faster in-database.

One subtle point: Dataiku's "Concat distinct" deduplicates exact string matches but is case-sensitive and whitespace-sensitive. If you want true semantic deduplication of categories, normalize the case and whitespace in the staging Prepare recipe before grouping (which you already did in step 6).

## 11. Safely join with left joins

Now create a Join recipe with three inputs: `incidents_staging` (left), `causes_aggregated` (right 1), `consequences_aggregated` (right 2). Output: `incidents_enriched`.

Configuration:

- **Join type**: Left join for both joins. This is non-negotiable — an inner join would silently drop incidents that have no causes or no consequences recorded, which is exactly the kind of data-quality gap you want to see, not hide.
- **Join keys**: `incidents_staging.incident_id = causes_aggregated.incident_id` and `incidents_staging.incident_id = consequences_aggregated.incident_id`.
- **Selected columns**: in the "Selected columns" tab, keep all columns from incidents_staging, and from the aggregated datasets keep only the aggregation outputs (not the redundant `incident_id`).
- **Post-filter**: none.

After the join, add a Prepare recipe `incidents_enriched` → `incidents_final` that:

1. Replaces nulls in the count columns with 0 (`cause_count`, `consequence_count` — null means "no causes recorded", which numerically should be 0).
2. Replaces nulls in the concatenated text columns with empty string.
3. Replaces nulls in the summed financial impact with 0 only if business semantics allow.
4. Adds derived flags: `has_causes_recorded` (cause_count > 0), `has_consequences_recorded`.

## 12. Validate one-row-per-incident

Build a Group recipe on `incidents_final` grouping by `incident_id` with a count aggregation, output `incidents_final_dq_check`. Then add a Prepare recipe with a filter `count > 1`. The result should be empty — zero rows.

If it's not empty, you have a bug in either the staging cleaning (duplicate incident IDs in the source) or the join (a Group recipe didn't actually deduplicate, usually because the group key column had trailing whitespace differences). Investigate immediately; do not proceed to NLP until this check passes.

Additionally, build three numeric checks as Dataiku **metrics** on `incidents_final` (Settings → Status → Metrics):

- Row count equals distinct count of `incident_id`.
- Row count equals row count of `incidents_staging` (the left join must preserve all left rows).
- No negative values in `gross_loss_eur` after sign convention is applied.

Promote these to **checks** (Settings → Status → Checks) so the build fails loudly if any of them break in the future.

## 13. Prepare the final NLP corpus

Now build the NLP-ready dataset. Add a Prepare recipe `incidents_final` → `incidents_nlp_corpus`. Steps:

1. Build a consolidated text column `nlp_text` using a Formula step. Concatenate, with explicit sentence-style separators that word2vec will treat as boundaries, the incident description, the aggregated cause descriptions, and the aggregated consequence descriptions. Something like: `concat(coalesce(incident_description, ""), ". CAUSES: ", coalesce(cause_descriptions_concat, ""), ". CONSEQUENCES: ", coalesce(consequence_descriptions_concat, ""))`. The literal "CAUSES:" and "CONSEQUENCES:" tokens will be removed in stop-word cleaning, but they prevent unrelated words from co-occurring across boundaries during embedding.
2. Filter out rows where `nlp_text` is shorter than some minimum (e.g., 20 characters) — these are uninformative.
3. Compute `nlp_text_char_len` and `nlp_text_word_count`. Look at the percentiles. If p99 is above ~1000 words and the median is below ~50 words, you have heterogeneous lengths and TF-IDF concerns from the paper apply — plan to test both TF and TF-IDF in the NLP step rather than committing to one.
4. Keep, alongside `nlp_text`, the structured columns useful for cluster validation: `basel_event_type_l1`, `risk_category`, `country`, `business_unit`, `gross_loss_eur`, `date_accounting`, `cause_categories_concat`, `consequence_categories_concat`, and the count columns.

The actual NLP cleaning (lowercasing, stop-words, lemmatization) I'd put in a Code recipe (Python) downstream of `incidents_nlp_corpus`, not in this Prepare recipe — because you'll iterate on it heavily and want it under version control as Python code, not as 30 Prepare steps.

## 14. Organize the Dataiku Flow with clear names

Use Flow zones (right-click in Flow → "Create zone") to visually group datasets. Suggested zones:

- **00_Raw**: `incidents_raw`, `causes_raw`, `consequences_raw`.
- **10_Staging**: `incidents_staging`, `causes_staging`, `consequences_staging`.
- **20_Aggregated**: `causes_aggregated`, `consequences_aggregated`.
- **30_Joined**: `incidents_enriched`, `incidents_final`.
- **40_DQ**: `incidents_final_dq_check` and any other validation datasets.
- **50_NLP_Corpus**: `incidents_nlp_corpus` (this is the handoff point to the NLP team, conceptually equivalent to the input of step 1 in the paper's workflow).
- **60_NLP_Workflow**: future zone for cleaned corpus, document-by-term matrix, semantic-aware matrix, LSA output, and clustering results.

Color the zones (Flow zones support colors) and pin a "Flow item" comment at the entry of each zone explaining its purpose. Future-you and your colleagues will thank present-you.

## 15. Checks and metrics before moving to NLP

On `incidents_nlp_corpus`, configure these as Dataiku metrics (auto-computed on every build) and as checks (build fails if violated):

**Volumetric metrics**: total row count, distinct incident count, count of rows with non-empty `nlp_text`, count of rows passing the minimum-length filter.

**Distributional metrics**: mean and p99 of `nlp_text_word_count`, mean and p99 of `cause_count` and `consequence_count`, distinct count of `basel_event_type_l1`.

**Integrity checks**: `incident_id` is unique (already validated in step 12, re-assert here), no nulls in `incident_id`, every row has `nlp_text_char_len > 0`, the sum of `gross_loss_eur` matches the sum from `incidents_staging` (the left join must not have changed totals).

**Methodological readiness check**: distinct count of incidents per Basel event type. The paper subset to one event type ("Clients, Products & Business Practices", 644 events) for tractable analysis and clean cluster interpretation. Look at your distribution: if one event type dominates with >500 events, you can replicate the paper closely on that subset; if all seven event types have <100 events, you may need to either pool them and accept noisier clusters, or run separate clustering per event type and compare.

Once these all pass, you have a clean, audit-able, one-row-per-incident dataset, and you're ready to start the actual workflow described in the paper — beginning with description cleaning, where you'll lemmatize, strip stop-words from a list like the Lingua::StopWords source the paper uses, and start building the document-by-term matrix.

One final pragmatic note: build the entire flow first with sampling enabled (Dataiku's "Sample" feature on each dataset) using the first 10,000 incidents only. The Group and Join recipes behave the same on samples as on full data, but iteration is 50× faster. Switch to full-data builds only after step 15's checks pass on the sample.
